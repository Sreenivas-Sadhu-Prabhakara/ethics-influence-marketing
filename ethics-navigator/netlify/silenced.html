The Omission

None of the open-ended questions Carrington proposed were included in the final survey. Every single opportunity for customers to speak freely was removed.

The Justification

Carrington's colleagues provided several reasons for excluding qualitative questions. Each excuse reveals a different aspect of the firm's compromised culture.

schedule

"Too Time-Consuming"

Qualitative questions require manual coding and analysis — the team argued it wasn't worth the effort.

sentiment_dissatisfied

"Clients Dislike Complaints"

Open-ended responses might surface unexpected complaints that clients don't want to read in their reports.

troubleshoot

"Could Derail Discussions"

Qualitative data could prompt clients to scrutinize the report more heavily, threatening the positive narrative.

shield

The Ethical Implication

By removing open-ended questions, the research team eliminated customer authenticity. Real customers with real frustrations were given no way to express them. The survey became a one-way mirror reflecting only what the client wanted to see.

What Ethical Research Looks Like

A properly designed survey balances quantitative and qualitative methods to capture the full picture.

Quantitative Questions

  • ✓ Use neutral, balanced scales (1–10)
  • ✓ Avoid embedded assumptions
  • ✓ Include full range of options
  • ✓ No leading or loaded phrasing

Qualitative Questions

  • ✓ Open-ended text fields
  • ✓ "Describe in your own words" prompts
  • ✓ Dedicated space for complaints
  • ✓ Follow-up probes for key issues

Discussion Questions

  1. Is omitting questions an equally serious ethical breach as altering them? Why or why not?
  2. How does removing qualitative feedback change the power dynamic between the researcher and the respondent?
  3. If a client explicitly asks for no open-ended questions, does the research firm have an obligation to push back?
  4. What mechanisms could prevent the systematic silencing of customer voices in market research?
